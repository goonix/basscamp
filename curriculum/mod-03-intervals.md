# Module 03 — Intervals: The Language of Distance

## Why Intervals Matter

An interval is the distance between two notes. Intervals are the vocabulary of music theory — once you understand them, everything else (scales, chords, bass lines, harmony) becomes describable and predictable.

For bass players specifically, intervals are how you navigate the fretboard without memorizing every note by name. Once you know what a perfect fifth looks and sounds like, you can find it anywhere instantly.

---

## Half Steps and Whole Steps

The half step is the smallest interval — one fret on the bass. The whole step is two frets.

```
G  |--0--1--|   ← half step (E to F)
G  |--0-----2|  ← whole step (E to F#)
```

All other intervals are built from combinations of half steps.

---

## The 12 Intervals

Starting from any note (the "root"), there are 12 possible intervals within one octave:

| Semitones | Interval Name | Abbreviation | Sound Character |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|-----------------|
| 0  | Unison | R | Same note |
| 1  | Minor 2nd | b2 | Tense, dissonant — one fret up |
| 2  | Major 2nd | 2 | Mild, stepwise — two frets up |
| 3  | Minor 3rd | b3 | Darker, minor feeling |
| 4  | Major 3rd | 3 | Brighter, major feeling |
| 5  | Perfect 4th | 4 | Open, strong — the interval between open strings |
| 6  | Tritone | b5 | Maximum tension — exactly halfway through the octave |
| 7  | Perfect 5th | 5 | Powerful, stable — the interval of power chords |
| 8  | Minor 6th | b6 | Rich, slightly dark |
| 9  | Major 6th | 6 | Warm, open |
| 10 | Minor 7th | b7 | Bluesy, unresolved — wants to move |
| 11 | Major 7th | 7 | Dreamy, tense — one half step below the octave |
| 12 | Octave | 8 | Same note, higher — fully resolved |

You don't need to memorize all of these today. The most important ones for bass are: the **root (R)**, **minor 3rd (b3)**, **major 3rd (3)**, **perfect 4th (4)**, **perfect 5th (5)**, **minor 7th (b7)**, and **octave (8)**. These are the chord tones and scale tones you'll use constantly.

## Intervals on One String

On a single string, every interval is just a fret count:

| Interval | Frets |
|----------|-------|
| Minor 2nd | 1 |
| Major 2nd | 2 |
| Minor 3rd | 3 |
| Major 3rd | 4 |
| Perfect 4th | 5 |
| Tritone | 6 |
| Perfect 5th | 7 |
| Minor 6th | 8 |
| Major 6th | 9 |
| Minor 7th | 10 |
| Major 7th | 11 |
| Octave | 12 |

Example: starting from A at fret 5 on the E string, a perfect 5th is 7 frets up — fret 12, which is E:

```
G  |----------------|
D  |----------------|
A  |----------------|
E  |--5----------12-|
      A           E
      Root        Perfect 5th
```

## Intervals Across Strings

Playing intervals across strings is more practical than stretching across 7+ frets on one string. The tuning (each string is a perfect fourth apart) creates consistent shapes.

### Perfect 5th shape (most important shape in bass)

```
G  |--2--|
D  |-----|
A  |--0--|   A to E — root on A string, 5th on G string
```

or

```
D  |--2--|
A  |-----|
E  |--0--|   E to B — root on E string, 5th on D string
```

The perfect 5th shape: **same fret, skip a string, move up 2 frets**. This works everywhere.

### Octave shape (from Module 01)

```
G  |--7--|
D  |-----|
A  |--5--|   A to A (octave)
```

Two strings up, two frets forward. You already know this one.

### Minor 7th shape

```
D  |--3--|
A  |--5--|   A to G — root on A string, minor 7th on D string
```

Same fret on the next string up. The minor 7th is the defining interval of dominant chords in blues and funk.

Knowing the names and shapes is only half the job. You need to **hear** intervals — to know what a minor 3rd sounds like before you play it.

Here are reference songs for each interval (starting from the first two notes):

## Why the Perfect 4th and 5th Are Special

The interval between each open string on your bass is a perfect fourth (5 half steps). This is why the fretboard patterns are so consistent — the same shape means the same interval everywhere.

The perfect 5th (7 half steps) is the most harmonically stable interval after the octave. It's the foundation of power chords, the first overtone in the harmonic series, and the most common interval in bass lines after the root. When in doubt, the root and fifth together always sound good.

---

## Applying Intervals: Root-5th Bass Lines

The most common bass pattern in rock and pop is root-fifth. Play the root on beat 1, the fifth on beat 3 (or in between):

```
G  |----------------|
D  |--2----------2--|
A  |--0----------0--|
E  |----------------|
    A  (root)   A  (root again, or continue to next chord)
       E (5th)     E (5th)
```

This works over any chord. It's simple, it sounds good, and it locks with the kick and snare perfectly.

---

## Ensemble Context

Understanding intervals changes how you hear music. When a guitarist plays a chord, you're not just hearing a wall of sound anymore — you can start to pick out the root (your note), the third (which tells you if the chord is major or minor), and the fifth (the power and stability).

As a bassist, the most harmonically loaded decision you make is whether to play the **root** (stable, supportive), the **fifth** (strong, powerful), or the **third/seventh** (color, tension). The root and fifth are safe everywhere. The third and seventh are what make bass lines musical and sophisticated.

That sophistication comes later. For now: know your roots and your fifths, and you're already doing 80% of what a bass player needs to do.
