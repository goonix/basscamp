# Module 02 — Rhythm Fundamentals for Bass

## Rhythm Is Your First Job

A bassist who knows every scale but can't lock with the drummer is useless in a band. A bassist who knows three notes but grooves hard is welcome everywhere. Rhythm comes first.

This module covers the building blocks of musical time — note values, time signatures, counting, and most importantly, how to lock with the kick drum. These aren't abstract concepts. Every idea here translates directly to how you play in a group.

---

## Note Values

Note values describe how long a note lasts relative to the beat.

| Note | Symbol | Duration in 4/4 |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| Whole note | 𝅝 | 4 beats |
| Half note | 𝅗𝅥 | 2 beats |
| Quarter note | ♩ | 1 beat |
| Eighth note | ♪ | 1/2 beat |
| Sixteenth note | 𝅘𝅥𝅯 | 1/4 beat |

In tab notation, the spacing between notes represents their rhythm. A quarter note groove looks like this:

```
G  |----------------|
D  |----------------|
A  |----------------|
E  |-0---0---0---0--|
    1   2   3   4
```

An eighth note groove doubles the density:

```
G  |----------------|
D  |----------------|
A  |----------------|
E  |-0-0-0-0-0-0-0-0|
    1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +
```

---

## Time Signatures

The time signature tells you how many beats are in each measure and what kind of note gets one beat.

**4/4** (common time) — 4 beats per measure, quarter note = 1 beat. This is the most common time signature in rock, pop, funk, R&B, and most other styles you'll play.

**3/4** (waltz time) — 3 beats per measure. COUNT: 1-2-3, 1-2-3.

**6/8** — 6 eighth notes per measure, grouped as two sets of three. Feels like a compound 2: ONE-two-three-TWO-two-three. Common in blues ballads and gospel.

Start with 4/4. Everything else can wait.

---

## Counting Subdivisions

The ability to feel where you are inside the beat is what separates groovy players from stiff ones.

### Quarter notes: count 1, 2, 3, 4

```
|  1  |  2  |  3  |  4  |
```

### Eighth notes: count 1-and, 2-and, 3-and, 4-and

```
| 1 + | 2 + | 3 + | 4 + |
```

The "and" falls exactly halfway between beats. If you tap your foot on 1-2-3-4, the "and" is when your foot lifts.

### Sixteenth notes: count 1-e-and-a, 2-e-and-a, 3-e-and-a, 4-e-and-a

```
|1 e + a|2 e + a|3 e + a|4 e + a|
```

Sixteenth notes are the currency of funk. Most funk bass lines live in the sixteenth-note grid.

**Practice tip:** Always count out loud when learning new rhythms. Counting internally is fine once you've internalized it, but out loud is faster for learning.

## Rests

A rest is silence — a specific amount of time where you don't play. Rests are as musical as notes. The space between notes creates groove.

| Rest | Duration |
|------|----------|
| Whole rest | 4 beats |
| Half rest | 2 beats |
| Quarter rest | 1 beat |
| Eighth rest | 1/2 beat |
| Sixteenth rest | 1/4 beat |

In tab, rests are written as dashes with no number:

```
G  |----------------|
D  |----------------|
A  |----------------|
E  |-0-------0------|
    1       3
```

This plays the root on beats 1 and 3, resting on 2 and 4. That's the foundation of a half-time feel — common in hip-hop and slow R&B.

## Locking with the Kick Drum

This is the most important concept in this module. In a band, the bass and kick drum form the **rhythm section foundation**. When they lock together, the groove feels inevitable. When they don't, everything feels loose and sloppy.

### The kick drum pattern in basic rock/pop

```
Kick:  X       X       X       X
       1       2       3       4
```

or more commonly:

```
Kick:  X           X
       1       2   3       4
```

(Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4 — the most common pattern in all of Western popular music.)

### Locking in practice

When the kick hits, **your note should hit at the exact same moment**. Not before, not after. Practice with a drum machine or metronome set to a simple kick pattern.

```
G  |----------------|
D  |----------------|
A  |----------------|
E  |-0-------0------|   ← bass hits on 1 and 3
    K       K           ← kick drum hits on 1 and 3
```

When you're locked, the bass and kick feel like one instrument — the listener can't quite separate them, and the groove feels deep and solid.

### The "glue" concept

The bass doesn't always have to play exactly what the kick plays — but it needs to be aware of it. Think of the kick drum as your rhythmic anchor. You can play around it, anticipate it, land with it — but you should always know where it is.

## Syncopation: A Preview

Syncopation means accenting the off-beats — the "ands" and "e's" — instead of (or in addition to) the main beats. It's what gives funk, R&B, and soul their characteristic push and pull feel.

A syncopated bass line anticipates beat 3 by landing on the "and" of 2:

```
G  |----------------|
D  |----------------|
A  |----------------|
E  |-0---0-0-----0--|
    1   2+3   3 4
```

We'll go deep on syncopation in Module 06 (Groove Architecture). For now, just be aware that not everything lands on the beat — and that's intentional.

## Practice Strategy

1. **Get a metronome.** A free app is fine. Set it to 60–80 BPM and don't turn it off.
2. **Count out loud** while you play. Always.
3. **Start slow.** A groove played perfectly at 70 BPM is worth more than a scrambled mess at 120.
4. **Tap your foot** on the quarter note beat while your hands play subdivisions. This coordination — foot on quarter notes, hands on eighth or sixteenth notes — is foundational.
5. **Listen to the kick drum** in every song you hear. Start noticing when the bass and kick lock together, and when they don't.

---

## Ensemble Context

In a band, tempo is a shared responsibility. You don't rush because you're excited; you don't drag because you're tired. The drummer sets the tempo; you lock with it.

**The cardinal rule:** when in doubt, lock with the kick on beat 1. You can't go wrong hitting beat 1 with the kick drum every single measure. From there, add complexity as you get comfortable.

A band where the bass and kick are locked can get away with a lot of sloppiness elsewhere. A band where the bass and kick are fighting each other sounds bad no matter what else is happening.
